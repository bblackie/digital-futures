## 3. Economics & Markets

### US Tariff Regime Generating $29 Billion Monthly But Squeezing Households

After the US Supreme Court struck down IEEPA-based tariffs in February 2026, the Trump administration invoked Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 to impose a new 10% global tariff on approximately $1.2 trillion (34%) of annual imports, effective February 24. The tariff regime is now generating an estimated $29 billion in monthly federal revenue, but the economic costs are being borne disproportionately by consumers: the average American household faces an estimated annual hidden tax burden of $1,050–$1,300 from tariff pass-throughs. Consumer discretionary earnings have dropped to levels not seen since the pandemic, and goods inflation is resurging. Global growth is projected at approximately 2.6% for 2026, with the IMF noting that while trade has proved more resilient than feared, economic fragmentation poses ongoing welfare risks worldwide.

## 4. Science

### UCLA Blood Test Detects Multiple Cancers and Organ Diseases From a Single Sample

UCLA scientists have published a study in *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* describing MethylScan, a low-cost blood test that analyses cell-free DNA methylation patterns to detect multiple cancers, liver diseases, and organ abnormalities simultaneously from a single blood draw. In early trials, the test detected approximately 63% of cancers across all stages and around 55% of early-stage cancers — figures the researchers acknowledge leave room for improvement but which are promising for a low-cost, broad-spectrum tool. The test represents a step toward routine multi-disease screening in primary care settings, particularly for populations without access to expensive targeted diagnostics. Separately, the Artemis II crew completed a historic lunar flyby on April 6, setting a new record for the farthest distance from Earth travelled by humans, surpassing Apollo 13's 248,655 miles, and are currently on their return journey to Earth.

## 7. Environment & Climate

### Health and Environmental Groups Sue Trump EPA Over Mercury Standards Rollback

A broad coalition including Earthjustice, the American Lung Association, the Natural Resources Defense Council, and the American Academy of Pediatrics filed a lawsuit in the US Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit challenging the Trump EPA's repeal of Biden-era amendments to the Mercury and Air Toxics Standards (MATS) for coal-fired power plants. The rollback, finalised in February 2026, effectively permits coal plants to emit more brain-damaging mercury, lead, arsenic, and nickel. Since their implementation in 2015, the original MATS standards had achieved a 90% reduction in mercury emissions from power plants, along with significant reductions in cancer risk, heart and lung disease, and premature death. A separate lawsuit was filed by a coalition of 21 states and local governments. The legal challenge is significant: Wisconsin joined the suit this week, expanding political and geographic support for the challenge.

### Gene Therapy Restores Hearing in People Born Deaf Within Weeks of Single Injection

In a small but landmark clinical study, researchers delivered a working copy of a critical hearing gene directly into the inner ear using a single injection, with patients born profoundly deaf reporting hearing restoration — in some cases within weeks. The therapy targets congenital deafness caused by mutations in a specific gene responsible for auditory hair cell function, and the speed of response has surprised researchers. While the study is small and longer-term outcomes remain to be established, it is regarded as the most significant advance in congenital deafness treatment to date. ## 11. Today I Learned

**TIL: The Artemis II crew witnessed a solar eclipse from the far side of the Moon — the first humans to do so in more than 50 years.**

As the Orion spacecraft passed behind the Moon during the April 6 lunar flyby, the crew experienced a nearly hour-long solar eclipse as the spacecraft, Moon, and Sun aligned. From that vantage point — the lunar far side, never visible from Earth — they also witnessed an "Earthrise" and "Earthset", observed impact craters, ancient lava flows, and surface ridges, and photographed terrain no human eye has studied at close range since Apollo 17 in 1972. The four-person crew (Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen) set a new human distance record, surpassing Apollo 13's mark of 248,655 miles from Earth.
