# 3D Modelling Project Guide

**Course:** 10DGTA  
**Unit:** 3D Modelling with Blender  
**Topic:** 3D Modelling Project (Practical Application)  
**Duration:** 1 week (Week 3, Term 1)  
**Aligned Outcome:** Designing & Developing Digital Outcomes—students follow a defined process to design, develop, and evaluate digital content in authentic contexts

---

## 1. Purpose of This Guide

This is your roadmap for completing a 3D modelling project from start to finish. You'll:
1. Choose or receive a modelling brief
2. Gather reference images
3. Build a 3D model using techniques from Units 1–4
4. Texture and light it
5. Render and present your final work
6. Reflect on your process

---

## 2. What You'll Create

A **3D model** of a real-world or imaginary object, complete with:
- ✅ Geometry (shape and detail)
- ✅ Materials and textures (color and surface properties)
- ✅ Lighting setup (professional three-point lighting)
- ✅ Final render (high-quality image)
- ✅ Process documentation (showing your iteration)

**Time estimate:** 5–8 hours of lab work across one week

**Scope examples:**
- A realistic coffee mug with handle, texture, and lighting
- A stylized robot character
- A mechanical gear or engine component
- A piece of furniture (chair, table, bookshelf)
- A plant or flower (organic shapes)
- A fantasy object (sword, shield, crown)

## 3. Project Timeline

### Day 1–2: Planning and Reference Gathering

**Task:** Define your project scope and find reference images.

**Steps:**

1. **Choose your object:**
   - Something you see every day (mug, phone, shoe, plant)
   - OR something from a brief provided by your teacher
   - OR something you imagine and can research (robot, vehicle, creature)

2. **Gather reference images:**
   - Find 3–5 images from multiple angles (front, side, top, back)
   - Use websites: Google Images, reference.pictures, concept art, photography
   - Save them to your project folder under `References/`

3. **Plan your approach:**
   - What basic shape will you start with? (box, cylinder, sphere)
   - What modeling technique will you use? (box modeling, sculpting, assembly of primitives)
   - Will you use materials and textures, or keep it simple?
   - Write 2–3 sentences in a project plan document

4. **Create your project folder:**
   ```
   MyObject_Project/
     ├── Blender_Files/
     ├── Textures/
     ├── References/
     ├── Renders/
     ├── Exports/
     └── Project_Plan.txt
   ```

5. **In your project plan, write:**
   - What are you modeling?
   - Why did you choose it?
   - What challenges do you expect?
   - What materials will you use?

### Day 3–4: Modeling (Block-Out and Detail)

**Task:** Create the 3D geometry.

**Steps:**

1. **Open Blender and create your basic shape:**
   - `Shift+A` → Mesh → [appropriate shape]
   - Or start with a cube and adjust

2. **Block out the basic proportions:**
   - Use `G` (grab), `S` (scale), `R` (rotate) to position
   - Reference your images constantly
   - This should take 30 min–1 hour
   - Save as `model_v1.blend`

3. **Enter Edit Mode (`Tab`) and add detail:**
   - Add loop cuts (`Ctrl+R`) where you need shape control
   - Extrude (`E`) to add features (handle, legs, details)
   - Scale faces to refine proportions
   - This should take 1–2 hours
   - Save as `model_v2.blend`

4. **Optional: Apply Subdivision Surface modifier**
   - Right panel → Modifier Properties → Add Modifier → Subdivision Surface
   - Set viewport division to `2`, render to `3`
   - This smooths your model non-destructively
   - Makes iteration easier

5. **Iterate:**
   - Check your model against references
   - Adjust proportions if needed
   - Add more detail if time allows
   - Save as `model_v3.blend`

**Checkpoint:** At end of Day 4, you should have a recognizable model with basic detail.

### Day 5: Materials, Lighting, and Rendering

**Task:** Make it look professional.

**Steps:**

1. **Create materials:**
   - Right panel → Material Properties → New
   - For each material (wood, metal, plastic, fabric):
     - Set Base Color
     - Adjust Roughness (0–1)
     - Set Metallic (0 = non-metal, 1 = metal)
   - Apply materials to your model

2. **Set up lighting:**
   - Delete default light (if desired)
   - Add three lights: Key, Fill, Back (Area Lights)
   - Position them using three-point lighting principles
   - Adjust power and color until object looks good

3. **Switch to Cycles renderer:**
   - Top-right, Render Engine → Cycles
   - Render Properties → Samples → 256 (for quality)

4. **Render:**
   - Press `F12` to render
   - Wait for render to finish (1–5 minutes depending on complexity)
   - If it looks good, save the render as `render_final.png`
   - If it doesn't, adjust materials/lighting and re-render

5. **Export your model:**
   - File → Export As → FBX or OBJ
   - Save to `Exports/` folder
   - Name it `modelname_final.fbx`

**Checkpoint:** At end of Day 5, you have a textured, lit, rendered model.

### Day 6–7: Process Documentation and Reflection

**Task:** Show your iteration and reflect on your learning.

**Steps:**

1. **Create a process document:**
   - Write 3–5 paragraphs describing:
     - Your initial concept and why you chose it
     - Your approach to modeling (what techniques you used)
     - Challenges you faced and how you solved them
     - What you'd do differently next time
     - What worked well and what you'd improve

2. **Collect screenshots:**
   - Take screenshots of `model_v1`, `model_v2`, `model_v3` (showing iteration)
   - Screenshot of your viewport (showing organization)
   - Screenshot of final render
   - Save these to `References/` or `Renders/`

3. **Organize your submission:**
   - Final Blender file (e.g., `model_final.blend`)
   - Final render image (e.g., `render_final.png`)
   - Process document (e.g., `Process_Report.txt` or `.md`)
   - Screenshots of iteration (optional but recommended)
   - Project plan (from Day 1)

4. **Reflection (to discuss with your teacher):**
   - What did you learn about 3D space and modeling?
   - What was harder than you expected?
   - What was easier than you expected?
   - How would you approach a similar project next time?

## 4. Assessment Criteria

Your project will be assessed on:

### Geometry (40%)
- ✅ **Accuracy:** Does the model match references? Are proportions correct?
- ✅ **Detail:** Is there appropriate detail for the brief scope?
- ✅ **Topology:** Do edge loops flow naturally? Is it organized?
- ✅ **Technique:** Did you use modeling techniques appropriately (extrude, loop cut, scale)?

### Materials and Lighting (30%)
- ✅ **Material realism:** Do materials look like their real-world equivalent?
- ✅ **Lighting:** Is the model well-lit from multiple angles? Does it look 3D?
- ✅ **Render quality:** Is the final render clear and professionally presented?

### Process and Iteration (20%)
- ✅ **File organization:** Are files and objects named clearly? Are versions saved?
- ✅ **Iteration:** Can you show multiple versions? Do you improve from v1 to v3?
- ✅ **Documentation:** Can you explain your process and decisions?

### Professionalism (10%)
- ✅ **Presentation:** Is your final render ready to share? Is it high-quality?
- ✅ **Backup:** Is your work safely stored with versions saved?
- ✅ **Explanation:** Can you describe what you did and why?

## 5. Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

### ❌ "I started without any references."
**Solution:** Spend 30 min finding and organizing reference images before you open Blender. Reference images are your guide.

### ❌ "I modeled for 2 hours then realized the proportions are all wrong."
**Solution:** Check against references every 15 minutes. Catch mistakes early.

### ❌ "I modeled for 8 hours straight. Now my model looks weird and I'm tired."
**Solution:** Take breaks. Model for 45 min, then step away for 10 min. Your eyes and brain need rest to assess proportion accurately.

### ❌ "I only saved one version. Then I made a huge mistake and can't undo it."
**Solution:** Save versions as you go. After finishing geometry, save `model_v1.blend`. After texturing, save `model_v2.blend`. Always have a rollback option.

### ❌ "My render is dark and noisy."
**Solution:** 
- Low light? Add more lights or increase power
- Noisy? Increase Cycles samples to 256+, but it will render slower

### ❌ "I can't make the model look interesting."
**Solution:**
- Lighting is 80% of the visual impact. If geometry is okay but render is boring, it's probably a lighting problem.
- Try three-point lighting with stronger key light, softer fill light.

### ❌ "I don't know what to model. I'm stuck."
**Solution:**
- Look around your room. Model something you see (lamp, pencil, bottle, pillow).
- Model your favorite object.
- Ask your teacher for a brief or constraint (e.g., "model something made of metal").

## 6. Tips for Success

### Before You Start
1. **Gather at least 3 reference images** from different angles
2. **Write a 1-paragraph project plan** so you know your scope
3. **Create your folder structure** and organize before opening Blender

### While You Model
1. **Reference constantly.** Pause every 10 min and compare your model to a reference image.
2. **Test your render early.** Don't finish the whole model, then render. Render after basic geometry, again after details, again after texturing. This catches mistakes early.
3. **Keep it simple.** You have 1 week. A well-modeled mug beats a half-finished chair.
4. **Save versions.** After major milestones (geometry done, textured, lit, rendered), save a new version.

### When You're Done
1. **Render multiple angles.** Front, side, three-quarter view. This shows your model in best light.
2. **Write your process report while you remember.** Don't wait until the last minute.
3. **Double-check file organization.** Is everything named clearly? Are backups safe?

### If You Get Stuck
1. **Check the Common Misconceptions section** in the relevant topic
2. **Rewatch the relevant tutorial video**
3. **Ask a classmate** to look at your model and give feedback
4. **Show your teacher** your current model and ask for advice
5. **Simplify the scope.** If you're running out of time, remove features, not quality.

## 9. Key Vocabulary

- **Block-out:** Early stage of modeling with basic shapes and proportions, not detail
- **Detail:** Secondary geometry that refines the form (after block-out)
- **Iteration:** Improving your model by making versions (v1, v2, v3)
- **Reference image:** Photo or artwork you use to guide your modeling
- **Three-point lighting:** Professional lighting setup with key, fill, and back lights
- **Render:** Calculate the final image of your 3D model
- **Export:** Save your Blender model in a different format (FBX, OBJ, etc.)
- **Process documentation:** Written description of how you made your project
- **Topology:** The arrangement of edges and vertices across your model
- **Proportions:** The relative sizes of different parts of your model (e.g., head size vs. body size)
