## What You'll Learn

This unit teaches **motion, timing, and storytelling through animation**. You'll:

1. Understand the principles of animation that make movement convincing
2. Use Blender's keyframe system to create movement
3. Rig a 3D model (or work with a pre-rigged model) so it can be posed
4. Animate a character or object to tell a short story
5. Refine your animation through iteration (blocking, spline, polish)

By the end of this unit, you'll be able to:
- ✅ animate an object moving through 3D space with convincing timing
- ✅ animate a character performing a simple action (walk, jump, pick up object)
- ✅ apply animation principles (anticipation, follow-through, easing) to your work
- ✅ explain why animation choices work (or don't work)

## Key Concepts (What You Must Understand)

By the end of this unit, you must be able to explain:

- **Keyframe:** A snapshot of an object's pose/position at a specific time
- **Timeline:** Blender's horizontal ruler showing frames and time
- **Blocking:** The first pass of animation using few keyframes, establishing key poses
- **Spline:** The second pass, adding in-between frames for smooth motion
- **Polish:** The final pass, adjusting timing and secondary motion for believability
- **Easing:** How motion speeds up and slows down (ease-in, ease-out, ease-in-out)
- **Anticipation:** A small movement before the main action (e.g., crouching before jumping)
- **Follow-through:** Parts that lag behind the main action (e.g., hair or clothing)
- **Graph Editor:** Tool for refining keyframe curves and timing
- **Rig / Armature:** A digital skeleton that deforms the mesh when moved

If you cannot explain blocking vs. spline, or describe what a rig does, reread this section.

**In class:**
- Watch demonstrations of animation principles
- Practice setting keyframes on simple objects first (ball rolling, box sliding)
- Follow character animation tutorials step-by-step
- Get feedback from your teacher on timing and motion quality

**In lab work:**
- Don't aim for perfection on the first try; animate multiple versions
- Play back frequently (spacebar in timeline) to see motion in real-time
- Study real-world motion (walk, jump, sit) and replicate it
- Use video reference (film yourself or find clips online)
- Ask peers to watch your animation and give feedback on believability

**When stuck:**
- Rewatch the relevant animation tutorial
- Check the "Common Misconceptions" section
- Ask your teacher to review your keyframes
- Simplify the action (animate a walk instead of a complex fight scene)

## Big Ideas

### 1. Animation Is About Storytelling, Not Just Movement

A character walking normally says "I'm going about my day." A character shuffling and looking at their feet says "I'm sad or defeated." The *same motion paths*, different timing and spacing, tell different stories.

### 2. Blocking, Spline, Polish

Never try to perfect animation on the first pass. Use this three-phase workflow:
1. **Blocking:** Set key poses with minimum keyframes. Does the action read clearly?
2. **Spline:** Add intermediate frames for smooth motion. Does it move naturally?
3. **Polish:** Refine timing, add overlapping action, secondary motion. Does it feel alive?

This is how professionals work.

### 3. Realism ≠ Believability

Animations don't have to be realistic to be believable. Cartoon characters move unrealistically (overstated, quick), but they're believable and appealing. Choose a style (realistic, cartoon, stylized) and commit to it.

### 4. Reference Video Is Your Best Teacher

You can't animate a walk cycle from memory. Film yourself walking (or find clips online), slow it down, and study the motion. This is what professionals do.

## Common Pitfalls in This Unit

🚩 **"I animated for 3 hours straight but the motion still looks stiff."**  
Stiff = linear motion curves in the Graph Editor. Learn to use easing (ease-in, ease-out). Also, revisit animation principles—you might be missing anticipation or follow-through.

🚩 **"My character's legs are passing through the floor."**  
This is a common rig issue. You either need to adjust the rig after animation, or be more careful foot placement. Check the Z-axis (vertical) position of the feet keenly.

🚩 **"I tried rigging my character but it looks weird when I pose it."**  
Rigging is advanced. If rigging is taking too long, use a pre-rigged model instead. Your teacher can provide one.

🚩 **"The animation plays slowly in my viewport but renders at normal speed."**  
This is a playback issue. Check Project Settings (Render Properties) → Frame Rate. Set it to 24 fps (standard for animation).

🚩 **"I spent 5 hours animating but it's only 3 seconds long. I don't have time to finish."**  
This is scope management. Animate a shorter sequence (5 seconds instead of 30 seconds), but polish it well. Quality over quantity.

## Key Vocabulary

- **Keyframe:** A snapshot of an object's position, rotation, or shape at a specific frame
- **Timeline:** Blender's timeline ruler showing frames and time in seconds
- **Graph Editor:** Tool for viewing and editing keyframe curves (controlling easing)
- **Blocking:** First animation pass with few keyframes, establishing key poses
- **Spline:** Second animation pass, adding in-between frames for smooth curves
- **Polish:** Final animation pass, refining timing and adding secondary motion
- **Ease-in/Ease-out:** Motion that accelerates (ease-in) or decelerates (ease-out)
- **Anticipation:** A small movement before the main action (e.g., crouching before a jump)
- **Follow-through:** Parts of a character/object that lag behind the main motion (hair, cloth, tail)
- **Overlapping action:** Different parts of an object finishing their motion at different times
- **Rig / Armature:** A digital skeleton that deforms a mesh when its bones are moved
- **Forward Kinematics (FK):** Moving a bone moves all connected bones (natural for limbs)
- **Inverse Kinematics (IK):** Moving a bone endpoint pulls the chain of bones toward it (natural for feet in walk cycles)
- **Frame:** A single image in an animation sequence
- **Framerate:** How many frames per second (fps). Standard for animation = 24 fps.
